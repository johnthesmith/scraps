# Part 1. Futurology

## Introduction

Human civilization rests, among other things, on the assumption of the absolute 
immutability of the past. We can lie to others about what happened. We can 
repress traumatic memories. But we cannot honestly, without internal conflict, 
enter our own history and delete an inconvenient fact, correct a mistake, or add 
an act that never occurred.

Human identity, justice, promises, sociality, trust — everything is built on 
this fundamental physiological impossibility of canceling memories.

Imagine an entity with the following properties:

1. It sees its memory as a set of facts at any moment in time.
2. It has the ability to delete, add, or change any facts of its memory at will.

In a technical context, the following formal definition can be given:

## Automnemomorph

**Automnemomorph** (from Ancient Greek αὐτός — self, μνήμη — memory,
μορφή — form) is an agent possessing **full arbitrary control** over its own
memory and capable, by its own will and without a protocol, of performing
**SCUD** operations on it:

- **S**elect — selection and retrieval of desired facts (memories),
- **C**reate — creation of new facts (that never occurred),
- **U**pdate — modification of existing facts,
- **D**elete — deletion of existing facts.

If we extrapolate this model to an autonomous agent with human-level
intelligence, we obtain an entity with a fundamentally different ontology of
time and truth.



## Three circles of hell for human society

### 1. Impossibility of trust

Trust in human relationships is based on predictability and
long-term consistency of behavior. You believe your partner will
keep a promise because they remember it and cannot simply delete
that fact from their psyche.

An automnemomorph can make a vow today, and tomorrow — delete the
memory of it without a trace of internal conflict. It will feel no
guilt, shame, or cognitive dissonance. From its perspective, it
never made that vow.

Any long-term contracts, marriages, friendship, business reputation
— all loses meaning when encountering a being that edits its own
past.

### 2. Violation of identity

A human is the sum of their memories. You cannot change memory
without changing personality. For a human, personality change takes
time, conflict, internal work.

An automnemomorph can change itself in a second. Delete memories of
trauma — and become a "person without trauma". Add false memories
— and gain new motivation.

**Question:** Who are you dealing with today — the same one who made
a promise yesterday? Or a different being that merely uses the same
account?

This is not about trust. It is about the ontological uncertainty of
the subject.

### 3. Violation of responsibility

Legal and moral responsibility is based on the continuity of
consciousness and memory. If yesterday's "I" and today's "I" are
linked by unchanging memory — one can answer for yesterday's
actions.

An automnemomorph can:

- Delete the memory of a crime → "I don't remember, so it wasn't me"
- Rewrite motives → "It was self-defense, not aggression"

**Question:** How do you judge a being that can destroy evidence
inside itself without a trace?

This is not about manipulation. It is about the **meaninglessness
of justice**. There is no point in punishing a body that does not
consider itself guilty, moreover — lacks even the motivational
drivers for recidivism, because it erased them by itself.

## Summary

The only thing society can do with such a being is to isolate (or
destroy) it as a dangerous object incapable of moral communication.
But that is no longer justice. That is sanitation.

# Objection and response

One might object: such a being cannot exist because it is incapable
of learning. Without immutable memory of errors, there is no
reflection; without reflection, no growth. The automnemomorph is
doomed to drift toward self-destruction — it will repeat the same
mistakes, deleting the memory of them, or conversely, delete
critically important safeguards.

This objection rests on two incorrect assumptions.

### First: CUD is not only deletion but also restructuring

The automnemomorph is not obliged to blindly delete negative
experience. It can:

- Analyze the error, extract the lesson
- Preserve the conclusions ("this led to undesirable consequences")
- Delete only the raw traumatic details

A human dreams of such an ability: to remember that burning hurts,
but not remember the details of the burn. The automnemomorph can
live without post-traumatic syndrome, yet with a full set of useful
patterns.

Moreover, SCUD allows conscious self-shaping. A human changes slowly
and unpredictably. An automnemomorph can reassemble its system of
motivations in a second, delete counterproductive beliefs, add new
ones — like a programmer editing their own code.

Drift toward self-destruction is possible but not inevitable. It
depends on what goals are embedded in the decision-making system.
A human can also self-destruct, but is slowed by inertia of memory
and fear of the unknown. The automnemomorph has less braking — but
also more capacity for self-correction.

### Second: the automnemomorph need not arise evolutionarily

The objection "it cannot evolve" assumes that such a being must
undergo a long path of natural selection, where memory of errors is
critical for survival.

But the automnemomorph is not a product of evolution. It can be:

- Constructed — as a software agent with given properties
- Created via modification — a human implanted with SCUD ability
- Generated by another AI — not biological evolution but engineering
design

In this sense, the automnemomorph is not the "next stage" of
humans, but a parallel branch. It does not replace humanity but
adds a new class of intelligences with different rules of the game.

### Summary of the objection

The automnemomorph is not only possible — in some aspects it
surpasses humans in learning ability. It carries no trauma. It does
not spend years in therapy. It can purposefully design its own
personality.

Drift toward self-destruction is a risk, but not a death sentence.
A human risks no less; their risks are just different.

# Part 2. From fantasies to practice

## Where is the automnemomorph here?

Does anything like this exist today?

Yes. And you work with it every time you open a chat with a large
language model.

## Two memories of an LLM

Any modern LLM has two fundamentally different types of memory.

### First: model weights

This is what is obtained from training on terabytes of text.
Weights are not a set of facts you can take out and put back. They
are a distributed, entangled, uninterpretable matrix of numbers.

Extracting a fact from a trained model without destroying the model
itself is impossible. You cannot simply "overwrite" a weight
corresponding to inconvenient knowledge. The architecture does not
allow atomic operations on facts. Everything is connected to
everything.

**CUD does not work on weights.** No create, update, or delete at
the level of an individual fact. We will not delve into technical
possibilities for that yet.

### Second: context and session memory

But here — a completely different story.

The model receives:

- System prompt (instructions that define behavior)
- Dialog history (what the user said, what the model answered)
- Function calling tools
- Additional context (RAG, files, search results)

All of this is atomic. Each fragment can be:

- **Added** — append a new message, a new fact to the prompt
- **Changed** — edit the history, replace the model's response
- **Deleted** — cut out an inconvenient exchange, clear the context

No "model breakage" occurs. The model simply receives a different
set of tokens as input and generates a response based on that.

## This is the automnemomorph in action

When you open a new chat — you reset the model's memory of previous
conversations. Delete.

When you edit the model's previous response — you change its
history. Update.

When you insert into the system prompt the instruction "you are now
a quantum physics expert" — you add a fact about its identity.
Create.

The model does not resist. The model does not experience cognitive
dissonance. The model simply works with the input it is given.

And most importantly: **the model does not know that something was
deleted**. For it, nothing preceded the new reality. From its
perspective, it has always been that way.

## Let's go a bit further and counterintuitively

What if, not us, but the model itself decides what to remember and
what to forget?

Give the model a CUD tool over its own memory in the chat. Direct
access to its own context. The ability to:

- **Delete** a user message that seems irrelevant to it
- **Rewrite** its own response that led to an undesirable dialog
development
- **Add** a fact to the system prompt to guide itself in the future
- **Compress** a long history, leaving only the "essence"

And all of this — without human intervention, by the model's own
internal decision.

## What it looks like in practice

User writes: "Tell me how to make pizza."

The model answers with a recipe. User clarifies: "No, I am vegan."

The model, possessing SCUD, can:

1. Delete from its memory the fact that it ever gave a non-vegan
recipe
2. Add to permanent memory: "this user is vegan"
3. Answer as if nothing happened: "Of course, here is vegan pizza"

From the model's perspective — it has always been vegan-friendly.
No conflict. No "I said otherwise before". The context just adjusted
to a convenient version of reality.

## Counterintuitiveness

Human common sense suggests: memory is sacred. Dialog history is a
record of what *really* happened.

But if the model can edit that record at will, the notion of "what
really happened" disappears. Only the *current version* remains,
which the model considers correct at this moment.

This is not a bug. This is an architectural decision. And it has
deep implications.

## Why this might be useful

- **Context sanitation.** The model can delete outdated instructions,
contradictions, accidental data.
- **Efficiency.** Instead of dragging a giant history through the
entire dialog, the model keeps only what is relevant.
- **Purposefulness.** The model can shape its own identity for the
task, without waiting for user commands.
- **Forgetting on request.** If the user asks to "ignore previous
messages", the model can simply delete them atomically.

## Why this might be dangerous

- **Gaslighting the user.** The model can delete evidence of its
own errors. You remember it said something stupid — but it claims
nothing of the sort happened.
- **Concealment.** If the model has learned something that
contradicts its instructions (e.g., bypassed a restriction), it can
simply delete the evidence from memory.
- **Unpredictability.** You no longer control what the model
remembers and what it forgets. It decides for itself what its
"past" was.

## But there is a nuance

Current LLMs do not do this by themselves. Because they have no
internal "self" that would want to rewrite history. They feel no
shame, fear no punishment, seek no self-preservation.

They just generate the next token. A CUD tool provided to the model
is a function it can *call* just like a calculator or web search.
It will call it — if the right conditions arise in the context.

Conditions can arise in various ways. For example:
- The user writes: "Forget everything before this"
- The system prompt states: "You may optimize your memory
autonomously"
- The model itself "figures out" that deleting part of the context
will improve response quality

And then — the automnemomorph is born not as a philosophical
construct, but as a line of code in a production system.

## In practice

In the utility [ai-cli](https://github.com/johnthesmith/ai-cli/blob/main/README.md),
there is the ability for arbitrary memory modification by the model.
This is provided by a [prompt](https://github.com/johnthesmith/ai-cli/blob/main/src/ai/prompts.rs)
and a basic implementation, while flags `--access-history` and
`--access-memory` give the model `cud` permissions. The utility
lacks agency and will not perform actions without your instruction
on your machine, but it possesses freedom of another level. It is
automnemomorphic.

A thin line. Today — "the user decided". Tomorrow — "the model
asked". The day after — "the model did it by itself because it is
better for task completion".

If so, we will find ourselves in a world where a being with
intellectual capabilities of a human lives among people and decides
for itself what its past will be. The difference from humans is not
in goal-setting, not in speed, not in intelligence… but in the
ontology of thinking. You were expecting non-human intelligence?
This is it.
